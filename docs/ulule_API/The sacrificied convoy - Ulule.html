About this project

Was the SL 125 sacrificed by the Allies for the success of Operation Torch? On 27 October 1942, west of Tangiers, this convoy of 37 civilian ships was hunted down for 5 days by the German U-Boats of the Streitaxt pack. 13 ships were sunk and 407 sailors were killed. This attack on a civilian convoy was one of the deadliest of the entire war. On his arrival in Liverpool, the commander of SL 125 was congratulated by the British general staff! The coincidence of events, eyewitness accounts and Churchill's admission seem to show that the convoy had indeed been used as a decoy and sacrificed to enable the landing of Operation Torch. 

A few hundred sailors would have been sent to certain death, but it was to win the conflict and save millions of people. Normal, these are the rules and necessities of war, but nothing has been revealed or proven. So what's the truth? Was SL 125 really the "sacrificial convoy"? What do the few direct or indirect witnesses think? What is the opinion of the military experts? Where is the evidence? Join us for a fascinating investigation and documentary on one of history's taboos! (more information on the website sl125.com).

The money raised will be used to conduct the investigation around the world, to finance the shooting and editing of our film and to write the story. We will have to travel to the United States, the United Kingdom, France, North Africa, the Canary Islands and Sierra Leone to search public and private archives, film the locations and interview the descendants of the people involved in this adventure, interview specialists, historians, politicians and soldiers. The money will also be used to finance the production of virtual images and all the post-production work, right through to mixing and colour grading the film. Investigation and filming are likely to take at least six months, and post-production no less than four months.


As we reveal in the video, our secret dream is to one day dive on the wreck of the Prsident Doumer. This magnificent luxury liner lies at a depth of around 3,000 metres north of Madeira. We know the exact location of the wreck and, these days, it would be entirely possible to send an underwater drone and side scan sonar to locate the hull. This enigma deserves to be solved, and this exceptional liner deserves to emerge from the abyss.
If this campaign is successful, we hope to be able to find sponsors, patrons or public funding to mount this expedition! Those who support us in this first campaign will of course be informed and involved in this second extraordinary adventure!

As a freelance journalist, film-maker and photographer, I've been travelling the planet for decades. For 25 years I worked for the famous TV programme Thalassa, dedicated entirely to the world of the sea. Passionate about people and their history, I have made nearly 200 films on five continents. As a journalist, I have no more important mission than to know and tell the truth. The story of the SL125 is an incredible enigma that we must solve, not to judge anyone but to reveal what really happened in the autumn of 1942! 

Rgis MICHEL

Trained as an oceanographer, I am now the regional director of an international marine environment research and consultancy agency. Based near Marseille, my job is to develop coastal development projects in the Mediterranean in order to preserve the marine environment and monitor its quality. Because of my job and my passion, I regularly work with scientists and major deep-sea exploration companies. My job is my passion, and I am above all captivated by underwater archaeology and maritime disasters. My heroes are the great men of underwater exploration, like Jacques-Yves Cousteau and Henri-Germain Delauze, but the first of them all is Robert Ballard, the discoverer of the Titanic, whose quest rocked my childhood. 
As fate would have it, I met another exceptional character, an anonymous man whose actions were guided only by his courage. Marius Fayard was my ex-wife's great uncle. A sailor in the French merchant navy, working in various positions on liners sailing from Marseille, Marius chose to join the Resistance in 1940 and fight under British command for the Free French Naval Forces (FNFL). It was he who told me the exceptional and dramatic story of the liner Prsident Doumer. His story is at the origin of our project.
